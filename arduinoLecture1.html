Arduino Lecture 1

Arduino Microcontroller Overview

Things to see on the micro controller:

  1. Central Processing Unit (CPU)
  2. I/O pins.
    • Pins with a ~ symbol.
    • Numbers next to them.
    • Pins with an A on them.
    • Pins with a Tx or Rx symbol.
    • Location of the 3V and 5V pin.
    • Location of the Ground pins.
  3. The USB port.

Te microcontroller that we will be working with during our project is the Arduino Uno R3. We will talk about what a microcontroller is, what the different parts of the microcontroller is and what their functions are. We will also do our first Arduino Program towards the end of this lecture.

Before I tell you what a microcontroller is I first want to talk about the different parts of the Microcontroller. It will be a simple overview.

CPU

The CPU is in a sense the brain of the microcontroller. The CPU does the calculations and controls the “decisions” of the Arduino once our program was uploaded on to it. The CPU is a chip. In our case the chip’s name is Arduino Atmega.

Pins

The pins are places where we can connect our lights, motors, wires (our circuits too). The CPU determines when these pins have a high or low output (wheter the light should be on or off); more on this later.

There are a variety of pins.

Let us look at the Microcontroller. Pay attention to the number and symbols written on the floor, next to the pins.

The first thing we should see is that they are all labeled differently. That is because the labels next to them is the name given to them and we will refer to them by their names when we start programing them.

The second thing I want you to see is that some pins have a number, a name, and other symbols like ~ Tx and Rx. These pins can be used as regular pins but also have special qualities to them that the others do not (more on this later).

The third thing we should see are the GND, 5V, 3V pins. We use this pin to provide power to the Arduino via a battery when we want to use our robot and not have it connected to the computer. The ground is also the negative side of the power, we need to connect at least one end of our circuit to this pin.

USB

The USB port is where we connect the microcontroller to our computer so that we can upload the compiled code onto the Arduino. (We write our code on our computer; our computer converts our code to 1’s and 0’s that then gets uploaded onto the controller).

Microcontroller

The microcontroller is everything, the CPU, the Pins everything put together. In our case it is called the Arduino Uno R3.
